Fraud vector analysis
Timing attack — mathematical proof of failure
Why partial tier adds fraud resistance
A fraudster gaming the partial tier (I×D = 36) receives only 0.5×c_day per triggered day. With a 7-day delay, they must predict a partial event 7+ days in advance. Chennai convective storms persist 6–18 hours. No 7-day forecast achieves sufficient precision for sustained partial-tier events. Expected value of partial-tier gaming: also negative.
What is basis risk?
Basis risk = the mismatch between the weather at the IMD station and the weather at the worker's actual location. The station is a fixed point; the worker is mobile. If a storm is concentrated in northern Adyar but the station is 3km south, the gauge may read 28 mm·hr⁻¹ while the worker experienced 38 mm·hr⁻¹ — a missed partial payout. Conversely, the gauge reads 52 while the worker was sheltered — an unearned full payout.

Mitigation strategies
Basis risk and the partial tier
The partial tier acts as a natural basis risk buffer. An event that is I×D = 52 at a worker's location but reads I×D = 44 at the nearest gauge still triggers a partial payout (50% c_day) rather than nothing. This ensures that edge-case spatial mismatches near the full/partial boundary result in at least partial compensation rather than zero. The financial impact of this buffer is small (adds ~2–3% to E[Payout]) and is already captured conservatively in the λ_P_max calibration.
Premium basis risk adjustment
No explicit premium loading for basis risk is required. The over-trigger risk (~6%) means the insurer occasionally pays out when the worker was not fully affected — this is the insurer's basis risk. The under-trigger risk (~7%) means the worker occasionally doesn't receive a payout they deserved — this is the worker's basis risk. These roughly balance, with a small net premium benefit to the insurer of ≈1–2% of expected claims. This is retained as part of the operations loading rather than returned, which is standard practice for parametric products.
